# Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 (CrPC)

## Basic Information
- **Full Name**: The Code of Criminal Procedure
- **Short Name**: CrPC or Cr.P.C.
- **Year of Enactment**: 1973
- **Effective Date**: April 1, 1974
- **Status**: REPLACED by Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS) on July 1, 2024
- **Jurisdiction**: All of India (except Jammu & Kashmir had its own until 2019)
- **Type**: Procedural Criminal Law (Adjective Law)
- **Total Sections**: 484 sections
- **Chapters**: 37 chapters
- **Schedules**: 5 schedules
- **PDF Source**: https://www.indiacode.nic.in/bitstream/123456789/15272/1/the_code_of_criminal_procedure%2C_1973.pdf

## Purpose and Scope
The Code of Criminal Procedure (CrPC) is the procedural law that governs the administration of criminal justice in India. It is the machinery through which the substantive criminal law (IPC/BNS) is implemented.

### Fundamental Principle
- **IPC/BNS**: Defines WHAT crimes are and their punishments (Substantive Law)
- **CrPC/BNSS**: Defines HOW to investigate, prosecute, try, and punish (Procedural Law)
- **Evidence Act**: Defines HOW to prove crimes (Rules of Evidence)

## What CrPC Governs

### 1. Police Investigation
- How crimes are investigated
- FIR registration procedures
- Powers of search and seizure
- Arrest procedures
- Evidence collection

### 2. Court Procedures
- Which court has jurisdiction
- How cases are filed and tried
- Trial procedures
- Evidence recording
- Judgment delivery

### 3. Rights Framework
- Rights of accused persons
- Rights of victims
- Rights of witnesses
- Bail provisions

### 4. Judicial Powers
- Powers of different courts
- Sentencing authority
- Appellate powers
- Revisional jurisdiction

### 5. Execution
- How sentences are executed
- Remission and commutation
- Parole and furlough

## Structure Overview
- **37 Chapters**: Organized by procedural stages
- **484 Sections**: Covering entire criminal justice journey
- **5 Schedules**: Forms, classifications, and supplementary provisions
- **Flow**: From crime occurrence to final execution of sentence

### Chapter V: Powers of Arrest (Sections 41-60A)
**ONE OF THE MOST IMPORTANT CHAPTERS**

#### Section 41: When Police May Arrest Without Warrant
Police officer can arrest without warrant:
1. **Cognizable offence** committed in presence
2. Reasonable complaint/information of cognizable offence
3. In possession of stolen property
4. Obstructs police officer
5. Proclaimed offender
6. Any person for whom reasonable suspicion exists
7. Released convict breaking conditions
8. Deserter from armed forces
9. Outside India offence

**Conditions** (added by amendment):
- For offences with punishment less than 7 years or only fine:
  - Cannot arrest if:
    - Person gives name and residence
    - No reason to believe false
    - No reason to believe person will abscond

#### Section 41A: Notice of Appearance (Added 2009)
- For offences punishable with less than 7 years
- Police officer shall issue **notice to appear**
- Person must appear before police officer
- If fails to appear without reasonable cause: Can be arrested
- **Major reform** to reduce unnecessary arrests

#### Section 41B: Procedure for arrest and duties of officer
- Officer making arrest must:
  1. Bear accurate, visible identification
  2. Prepare memo of arrest
  3. Get attested by witness (family member or respectable person)
  4. Counter-sign by arrested person
  5. Inform person of right to have someone informed

#### Section 41C: Offence against police by arrested person

### Chapter IX: Order for Maintenance of Wives, Children and Parents (Sections 125-128)

#### Section 125: Order for Maintenance (VERY IMPORTANT)
**One of most invoked sections**

**Who Can Claim**:
1. **Wife** unable to maintain herself
2. **Legitimate/illegitimate minor children** (below 18)
3. **Major children** (if disabled/unable to maintain)
4. **Parents** unable to maintain themselves

**Against Whom**:
- Husband (for wife maintenance)
- Father (for children maintenance)
- Children (for parent maintenance)

**When Claim Can Be Made**:
- Wife:
  - Husband neglecting to maintain
  - Refusing to maintain
  - Has sufficient means but not maintaining
- Children: Father not maintaining
- Parents: Children not maintaining

**Amount**:
- Monthly allowance as magistrate thinks fit
- Not exceeding ₹500 per month (can be higher by state amendment)
- Considering means of husband/father/children

**Important Points**:
- Wife not entitled if:
  - Living in adultery
  - Refuses to live with husband without sufficient reason
  - Living separately by mutual consent
- Magistrate First Class can order
- Civil remedy in criminal court
- Quick and accessible

**Section 125(3)**: If husband fails to pay
- Warrant to levy amount
- Imprisonment: 1 month for each month default (max 6 months per order)

**Section 125(4)**: Alteration of allowance

### Chapter XII: Information to Police and Investigation (Sections 154-176)
**MOST CRITICAL CHAPTER - Core of Criminal Justice System**

#### Section 154: First Information Report (FIR)
**MOST IMPORTANT SECTION IN CrPC**

**What is FIR**:
- First Information of cognizable offence
- Received by police station
- Reduced to writing
- Foundation of criminal investigation

**Procedure**:
1. Any person gives information of cognizable offence orally
2. Officer in charge **must reduce to writing**
3. Read over to informant
4. Informant **signs**
5. Substance entered in book (General Diary)
6. **Free copy** given to informant

**Key Points**:
- Can be filed by anyone (victim, witness, or any person)
- Police cannot refuse to register FIR for cognizable offence
- FIR is not substantive evidence but can corroborate/contradict
- Can be registered on phone call in emergencies
- Time and date of registration critical

**What if Police Refuses**:
- Approach Superintendent of Police
- Send written complaint by post (deemed FIR)
- Approach Magistrate under Section 156(3)
- File writ in High Court

**Non-Cognizable Cases**:
- Police enters in diary
- Refers to magistrate
- Cannot investigate without magistrate's order

#### Section 155: Information About Non-Cognizable Offences
- Police cannot investigate
- Must refer to magistrate
- Informant can approach magistrate directly

#### Section 156: Power of Police to Investigate
**Critical provision**

**Section 156(1)**: Police officer can investigate cognizable offence:
- Without order from magistrate
- Independent power
- Cannot be prevented

**Section 156(3)**: Magistrate empowered under Section 190:
- Can order investigation by police
- Into cognizable offence
- Even if no FIR filed
- **Powerful tool for complainants** if police refuses FIR

#### Section 157: Procedure for Investigation
On receiving information of cognizable offence:
1. **Proceed in person** to spot (or depute subordinate)
2. If no sufficient ground: Do not investigate
3. If sufficient ground: Investigate

Steps:
- Ascertain facts and circumstances
- Take measures to discover and arrest offender

Report to magistrate:
- Immediately if crime committed
- Details of offence

#### Section 158**: Dispatch of copy of entry to magistrate

#### Section 159**: Power of police officer to require attendance
**Section 160: Police Officer's Power to Require Attendance of Witnesses

**Who Can Be Summoned**:
- Any person acquainted with facts/circumstances
- Whose attendance necessary

**Obligation**:
- Person **must attend**
- At place and time specified
- Failure can lead to arrest

**Protections**:
- Woman not required to attend police station
- Officer must go to her residence
- Cannot require attendance of person under 15 years at police station

#### Section 161: Examination of Witnesses by Police
**VERY IMPORTANT**

**Procedure**:
- Police can examine **orally** any person:
  - Acquainted with facts of case
  - During investigation

**Important Points**:
1. Person **bound to answer** all questions (except incriminating)
2. **Not on oath**
3. Statement **cannot be signed** by person
4. Person cannot be arrested for refusing to answer (unless already arrestable)

**Section 161(2)**: Person cannot be compelled to answer if:
- Answer would expose him to criminal charge
- Right against self-incrimination

**Use of Statement**:
- Not substantive evidence
- Can be used to **contradict witness** in court (Section 162)
- Can be used to corroborate

#### Section 162: Statements to Police Not to Be Signed
**Protection against manipulation**

**Rule**: Statement made to police during investigation:
- Shall **not be signed**
- Cannot be used as evidence (except for contradiction)

**Exception**: Statement to magistrate admissible (if Section 164)

#### Section 163: No Inducement to Be Offered
- Police/authority cannot offer:
  - Inducement
  - Threat
  - Promise
- To get confession/statement

**If violated**: Statement inadmissible

#### Section 164: Recording of Confessions and Statements
**By Magistrate**

**Who Can Record**:
- Metropolitan Magistrate
- Judicial Magistrate
- During investigation or after
- Before commencement of inquiry/trial

**Procedure**:
1. **Must explain** to person:
   - Not bound to make confession
   - If confesses, can be used as evidence
2. **Must ask** if confession voluntary
3. If appears involuntary: Cannot record
4. Record in question-answer form
5. Magistrate **signs**
6. Person **signs**
7. Magistrate must forward to concerned magistrate

**Important**:
- Protects voluntariness
- Such confession admissible (unlike police confession)
- Used in serious cases

#### Section 165: Search by Police Officer
During investigation, can search:
- Any place entered by person sought to be arrested
- Any place where stolen property suspected
- Within jurisdiction or with warrant outside

#### Section 166: Information to be Given of Grounds of Search
Must inform person before search:
- Grounds of search

#### Section 167: Procedure When Investigation Cannot Be Completed in 24 Hours
**CRITICAL SECTION - REMAND PROVISION**

**The Problem**: Section 57 says cannot detain beyond 24 hours
**The Solution**: Section 167 provides for magistrate's remand

**Procedure**:
If investigation not completed within 24 hours:
1. Police produces accused before magistrate
2. Magistrate may authorize **detention** (remand)
3. Two types:
   - **Police custody**: For investigation
   - **Judicial custody**: In jail, no investigation

**Police Custody Limits**:
- Maximum **15 days at a time**
- Magistrate can grant in parts
- Total police custody as per overall limits

**Total Detention Limits**:
- **60 days** for offences punishable with less than 10 years
- **90 days** for offences punishable with:
  - Death penalty
  - Life imprisonment
  - 10 years or more

**After 60/90 Days**:
- If charge sheet not filed
- Accused entitled to **default bail** (bail as of right)
- Magistrate must release on bail

**Important Points**:
- Magistrate must record reasons for remand
- Cannot be mechanical
- Must apply mind
- Accused can be heard
- First remand usually 14-15 days
- Subsequent remands scrutinized more

#### Section 168: Report of Investigation by Police**

#### Section 173: Report of Police Investigation (Charge Sheet/Final Report)
**FINAL STEP OF INVESTIGATION**

**Types of Reports**:
1. **Charge Sheet**: If sufficient evidence, accused charge-sheeted

**Documents Forwarded**:

**Important**:

- So charge sheet generally filed within 60/90 days

**Section 173(2)**: Further investigation possible

### Chapter XIV: Conditions Requisite for Initiation of Proceedings (Sections 190-199)

#### Section 190: Cognizance of Offences by Magistrates
**CRITICAL SECTION**

**How Cognizance Taken**:
Magistrate can take cognizance of offence:
1. Upon receiving **complaint**
2. Upon **police report** (charge sheet)
3. Upon **information** received from any person
4. Upon **own knowledge or suspicion**

**Cognizance Meaning**:
- Taking judicial notice of offence
- Deciding to proceed with case
- Not just receiving complaint
- Application of mind to proceed

**Important**:
- Chief Judicial Magistrate can take cognizance of any offence
- Magistrate First Class: cognizance of any offence (within jurisdiction)
- Magistrate Second Class: limited cognizance

#### Section 191**: Transfer on application of accused

#### Section 192-195**: Cognizance of offences by courts of session

#### Section 196**: Prosecution for offences against state

#### Section 197: Prosecution of Judges and Public Servants
**IMPORTANT PROTECTION**

**Provision**:
- When public servant accused
- Of offence alleged to be committed:
  - **While acting in official capacity**
  - **In discharge of official duty**
- No court can take cognizance **without sanction**

**Who Grants Sanction**:
- Central Government (for central employees)
- State Government (for state employees)
- Appropriate authority

**Purpose**:
- Protect public servants from frivolous prosecution
- Allow fearless discharge of duty
- Not blanket immunity

**When Not Required**:
- Acts done outside official capacity
- Purely private acts
- Acts not in discharge of duty

#### Section 198: Prosecution for Offences Against Marriage
- Offences like bigamy, cruelty
- Requires complaint from:
  - Aggrieved party (husband/wife)
  - Parent or relative if person unable
- Magistrate cannot take cognizance otherwise

#### Section 199: Prosecution for Defamation
- Requires complaint from:
  - Person defamed
  - If dead: certain relatives can complain
- Court cannot take cognizance suo moto

### Chapter XXV: General Provisions as to Inquiries and Trials (Sections 353-365)

#### Section 353-354: Judgment

**Section 353**: Judgment procedure

**Section 354: Judgment (CRITICAL SECTION)**
**Most important post-trial provision**

**Contents of Judgment**:
Must contain:
1. **Point for determination**
2. **Decision thereon**
3. **Reasons for decision**
4. If conviction:
   - Offence of which convicted
   - Section number
   - Punishment awarded

**Delivery**:
- In **open court**
- Immediately after trial conclusion
- Or some subsequent day (if complex)
- Accused must be present (if convicted)

**Language**:
- Language of court
- Or if accused doesn't understand: Explained in language he understands

**Special Points**:
- Judgment must be reasoned
- Cannot be cryptic
- Must discuss evidence
- Must give clear findings

**Section 354(3)**: Reasons for sentence
- When awarding sentence
- Special reasons if:
  - Awarding less than minimum
  - Awarding death instead of life
  - Not awarding compensation to victim

**Section 355**: Judgment copies
**Section 356**: Judgment procedure in other cases
**Section 357**: Order to pay compensation (see below)
**Section 358-365**: Other judgment provisions

### Section 357: Compensation to Victims (VERY IMPORTANT)

#### Section 357: Order to Pay Compensation and Expenses
**Victim Relief Provision**

**When Court Can Order**:
- When imposing fine
- In addition to or in lieu of fine
- Court can order:
  1. Whole or part of fine as **compensation to victim**
  2. For any loss/injury caused
  3. Payment for medical expenses

**Who Can Get**:
- Person who suffered loss/injury
- Legal representative if person died

**Amount**:
- From fine amount collected
- Reasonable amount
- Considering loss/injury

**Default**:
- If fine not paid: Compensation not paid
- But can recover as fine

**Important**:
- **Immediate relief to victim**
- No separate civil suit needed
- Quick and accessible

#### Section 357A: Victim Compensation Scheme (Added 2009)
**Major Reform**

**State Must Prepare Scheme**:
- Every state must prepare scheme
- For compensation to victims/dependents
- Who suffered loss/injury as result of crime
- Who require rehabilitation

**When Compensation**:
1. **Court recommends** (Section 357A(1))
2. Where accused not traced/identified
3. Where trial ends in acquittal/discharge
4. Compensation not awarded under Section 357

**Who Pays**:
- **State government** from Victim Compensation Fund

**Process**:
- Court may recommend
- Or victim can apply to District/State Legal Services Authority
- Authority decides compensation

**Types of Compensation**:
- Medical expenses
- Rehabilitation
- Loss of earning
- Pain and suffering

**Important**:
- Compensation even if accused not convicted
- State's duty to compensate victims
- Welfare state obligation

### Chapter XXVI: Provisions as to Bonds (Sections 360-371)

**Section 360: Order to Release on Probation of Good Conduct**
**REFORMATIVE JUSTICE**

**When Applicable**:
If person convicted of offence:
- Punishable with fine only, OR
- Not punishable with death or life imprisonment
- And person:
  - Under 21 years of age, OR
  - First offender
  - Court believes not to imprison having regard to:
    - Youth, character, antecedents
    - Circumstances of offence
    - Any extenuating circumstances

**What Court Can Do**:
1. **Release on probation** (instead of sentencing)
2. Release after **admonition** (warning)
3. Release on executing **bond** (with or without sureties)
4. For good behavior
5. For period as court thinks fit

**Conditions**:
- Good behavior bond
- Can impose conditions (report to probation officer, avoid certain places, etc.)
- If violates: Can be brought back and sentenced

**Purpose**:
- **Avoid jail** for young/first offenders
- Give chance for reform
- Avoid stigma of imprisonment
- Reformative approach

**Section 361**: Power to require security for good behavior
**Section 362-371**: Bond procedures

### Chapter XXVII: Provisions as to Bail (Sections 436-450)
**ONE OF THE MOST IMPORTANT CHAPTERS**

#### Fundamental Concepts

**Bail**: Provisional release of accused person
- On executing bond (with or without sureties)
- That person will appear before court when required
- And comply with conditions

**Types of Bail**:
1. **Regular Bail** (Section 437): After arrest
2. **Anticipatory Bail** (Section 438): Before arrest
3. **Default Bail** (Section 167): After 60/90 days in custody

**Bond**: Written undertaking
**Surety**: Person who guarantees accused will appear

#### Section 436: Bail in Bailable Offences
**Bail as of Right**

**Rule**: If person arrested or detained **without warrant**:
- For **bailable offence**
- And appears before officer/court
- Shall be **released on bail** (officer/court must release)

**When Bailable**:
- Offences in Schedule I marked as "bailable"
- Generally: Offences punishable with less than 3 years or fine only

**No Discretion**:
- Court/police cannot refuse bail
- Must release on bail
- If person executes bond (with or without sureties)

**Amount of Bond**:
- Reasonable
- Not excessive
- Considering circumstances

**Section 436A**: Maximum period for detention of undertrial prisoner
- If person undergone detention (as undertrial):
- For period extending up to **half of maximum sentence**
- Shall be released on **personal bond**

#### Section 437: Bail in Non-Bailable Offences
**Bail at Court's Discretion**

**General Rule**: No automatic right to bail

**When Court May Grant Bail**:
If reasonable grounds to believe:
- Accused **not guilty** of such offence
- Not likely to commit another offence while on bail

**When Court May Not Grant Bail**:
If there are reasonable grounds to believe accused guilty of offence:
- Punishable with **death or life imprisonment**

**Special Cases** (Section 437(1) Proviso):
Court may grant bail even in death/life imprisonment cases if accused is:
1. **Woman**
2. **Child** (below 16 years)
3. **Sick or infirm**
4. Accusation appears **doubtful**

**Factors to Consider** (not exhaustive):
1. Nature and gravity of offence
2. Character of accused
3. Severity of punishment
4. Danger of accused absconding
5. Likelihood of offence being repeated
6. Reasonable apprehension of witness being tampered
7. Larger interest of public/state

**Conditions**:
Court can impose conditions:
- Deposit passport
- Not to leave city/state
- Report to police station
- Not contact witnesses
- Not leave country
- Any other reasonable condition

**Section 437(2)**: Person released on bail
- If fails to comply with conditions
- Or is involved in similar offence
- Bail can be cancelled

**Section 437(3)**: Special provision for offences under Section 376, 376A, 376B (rape)
- Generally bail difficult
- Court must hear Public Prosecutor

**Section 437(4)**: Power to impose special conditions
**Section 437(5)**: Release of poor person unable to furnish bail
**Section 437(6)**: Conditions while granting bail (security for good behavior)

#### Section 438: Anticipatory Bail
**PRE-ARREST BAIL - UNIQUE PROVISION**

**When Applicable**:
- Person has reason to believe:
  - May be arrested for **non-bailable offence**
  - For accusation of having committed offence

**Where to Apply**:
1. **High Court**, OR
2. **Court of Session**
(Cannot apply to Magistrate)

**What Court Can Do**:
- If satisfied
- May issue direction:
  - If person arrested: shall be released on bail

**Conditions**:
Court can impose conditions:
1. Make himself available for interrogation
2. Not leave India without permission
3. Not directly/indirectly contact witnesses
4. Any other condition as court thinks fit

**Duration**:
- Generally until charge sheet filed
- But can be for longer period
- Or even throughout trial (rare)

**Important Points**:
- **Protection from arrest**
- Person gets bail immediately upon arrest
- Very useful for anticipating arrest
- Prevents custodial interrogation
- Prevents harassment

**When Not Granted** (Generally):
- Serious offences
- Likelihood of tampering with evidence
- Likelihood of absconding
- Past criminal record
- Gravity of accusation

**Section 438(2)**: Conditions of such bail
**Section 438(3)**: When person released on anticipatory bail
**Section 438(4)**: Anticipatory bail direction can be canceled

#### Section 439: Special Powers of High Court or Court of Session
**Superior Courts' Bail Powers**

**Powers**:
High Court or Sessions Court has power:
1. To grant **bail** to person:
   - In custody
   - For non-bailable offence
2. To **cancel bail** granted by:
   - Itself
   - Lower court
   - Police

**Special Features**:
- Can grant bail in **any case**
- Even if Magistrate refused
- Even in most serious cases
- Discretionary power
- Appeal against Magistrate's bail refusal

**Section 439(2)**: Power to cancel bail
- If person released on bail:
  - Misuses liberty
  - Commits another offence
  - Tampers with evidence
  - Threatens witnesses
- High Court/Sessions Court can cancel bail

**Section 446**: Conditions of bond

### Chapter XXIX: Appeals (Sections 372-394)
**RIGHT TO APPEAL**

#### Section 372-373: No Appeal in Certain Cases
**Section 374: Appeal from Convictions (CRITICAL)

**Who Can Appeal**:
Any person convicted by:
1. **Sessions Court or Additional Sessions Judge**: Appeal to **High Court**
2. **Magistrate**: Appeal to **Sessions Court**

**Time Limit**:
- **30 days** from date of conviction
- Can be extended if sufficient cause (up to 90 days)
- Beyond 90 days: Need special leave

**What Can Be Appealed**:
- Conviction
- Sentence
- Both

**On What Grounds**:
- Error of law
- Error of fact
- Error in procedure
- Sentence excessive
- Any ground that made trial unfair

#### Section 377: Appeal by State Against Acquittal
**State's Right to Appeal**

**When State Can Appeal**:
1. Against **acquittal** order
2. Against **inadequate sentence**
3. To High Court

**Who Can File**:
- State Government
- Through Public Prosecutor

**Important**:
- State can challenge acquittal
- But acquitted person already free
- If High Court convicts: Person recalled

#### Section 378**: Appeal by victim (limited)
Through state government

#### Section 379-394**: Appeal procedures, powers of appellate court, etc.

  2. Release appellant on bail

### Chapter XXX: Reference and Revision (Sections 395-405)

#### Section 395-396: Reference to High Court
**Section 397: Calling for Records to Exercise Powers of Revision (IMPORTANT)

**High Court/Sessions Court Power**:
- Can call for records of any proceedings
- Before any inferior court
- For purpose of:
  1. Satisfying itself as to **correctness**
  2. **Legality**
  3. **Propriety** of any finding, sentence, order

**Suo Moto** (On its own):
- Court can take suo moto cognizance
- On reading news report
- On complaint
- On own knowledge

#### Section 401: High Court/Sessions Court Powers of Revision

**Powers** (Section 401(1)):
Can:
1. **Enhance or reduce** sentence
2. **Alter nature** of sentence
3. **Set aside** finding/sentence
4. **Order retrial**
5. **Modify** any order

**Limitation**:
- Cannot enhance sentence or reverse acquittal:
  - Unless parties heard
  - Or had opportunity to be heard

**Important**:
- **Supervisory jurisdiction**
- Ensures correctness of orders
- Can correct errors

**Section 401(3)**: Sessions Court limitation
- Cannot enhance sentence beyond its powers

**Section 401(4-5)**: Other provisions

#### Section 482: Saving of Inherent Powers of High Court (MOST POWERFUL)

**Inherent Powers**:
Nothing in this Code shall limit:
- **Inherent powers** of High Court
- To make such orders as necessary:
  1. To give effect to any order under this Code
  2. To **prevent abuse of process** of any court
  3. To **secure the ends of justice**

**When Used**:
- To quash FIR (if frivolous, vexatious, or no offence)
- To quash proceedings (if gross abuse)
- To prevent miscarriage of justice
- To protect innocent persons
- When no case made out

**Famous Uses**:
1. **Quashing FIR** if:
   - No cognizable offence made out
   - Allegations don't constitute offence
   - Complaint frivolous/vexatious
   - Civil dispute, not criminal
   - Complaint for private vendetta
   - Settlement reached between parties (in compoundable cases)

2. **Quashing charge sheet/proceedings**
3. **Staying proceedings** (in exceptional cases)
4. **Preventing abuse of process**

**Important Points**:
- **Most frequently invoked**
- Very wide powers
- Can be used at any stage
- No limitation on powers
- Purpose: Secure justice

**Cannot Be Used**:
- To appreciate evidence (that's trial court's job)
- To usurp appellate powers
- For fishing and roving inquiries
- When disputed questions of fact

## Transition to BNSS

### Critical Date: July 1, 2024

**Before July 1, 2024**:
- All procedures under CrPC
- CrPC sections cited
- CrPC forms used

**On/After July 1, 2024**:
- All NEW procedures under BNSS
- BNSS sections cited
- BNSS forms used

### For Ongoing Cases
- **FIR filed before July 1**: CrPC procedures continue till case ends
- **Investigation started**: Complete under CrPC
- **Trials ongoing**: Continue under CrPC
- **Appeals pending**: CrPC applies

## Advantages CrPC Had
1. **50 years of jurisprudence**: Extensive case law
2. **Well-understood**: Lawyers, police, judges familiar
3. **Tested provisions**: Worked in various scenarios
4. **Comprehensive**: Covered all aspects

## Limitations That Led to BNSS
1. **No technology integration**: Manual processes
2. **No fixed timelines**: Led to delays
3. **Not victim-friendly**: Limited victim rights
4. **Colonial era law**: Needed modernization
5. **No forensic mandate**: Quality issues in investigation
