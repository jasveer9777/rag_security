# Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023 (BSA)

## Basic Information
- **Full Name**: Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (Indian Evidence Act)
- **Year of Enactment**: 2023
- **Act Number**: Act No. 47 of 2023
- **Effective Date**: July 1, 2024
- **Status**: CURRENT LAW (Active)
- **Jurisdiction**: All of India
- **Type**: Law of Evidence
- **Total Sections**: 170 sections
- **Chapters**: 11 chapters
- **PDF Source**: https://home.jk.gov.in/pdf/3BSA2023.pdf

## Purpose and Scope
BSA replaces the Indian Evidence Act 1872, modernizing evidence law for the digital age. It governs what evidence is admissible in court and how facts can be proved in criminal and civil proceedings.

## Major Changes from IEA

### 1. Digital Evidence Recognition (BIGGEST CHANGE)
- **Electronic Records**: Full legal recognition, same weight as paper
- **Digital Signatures**: Admissible as evidence
- **Email/SMS**: Explicitly covered and admissible
- **Social Media**: Posts, messages, chats admissible
- **Server Logs**: Can be used as evidence
- **Digital Documents**: No need for complex certificates (simplified Section 65B)
- **Screenshots**: Admissible with proper authentication
- **Audio-Video Recordings**: Explicit provisions for digital recordings

### 2. Simplified Language
- Victorian-era English replaced with modern language
- Hindi and English both simplified
- Easier for common people to understand
- Legal jargon reduced

### 3. Technology-Neutral Provisions
- Future-proof for new technologies
- Doesn't require updates for new digital formats
- Broad definitions cover emerging tech

### 4. Streamlined Procedures
- Removed Section 65B certificate requirement (major relief)
- Electronic evidence easier to admit
- Less procedural complexity
- Faster evidence production

## Structure
- **11 Chapters** covering all aspects of evidence
- **170 Sections** (3 more than IEA for clarity)
- Organized by: relevancy → admissibility → proof → witnesses

## Key Chapters

### Chapter I: Preliminary (Sections 1-2)
- Title, extent, commencement
- Definitions and interpretations

### Chapter II: Relevancy of Facts (Sections 3-54)
- **What facts can be proved**
- **When facts become relevant**
- Core principle: Only relevant facts admissible
- Sections 3-54 define relevancy in various situations

**Important Sections**:
- **Section 5**: Evidence of facts in issue and relevant facts
- **Section 6-16**: How facts become relevant (motive, preparation, conduct)
- **Section 17-23**: Admissions (statements accepting facts)
- **Section 24-30**: Confessions (admitting guilt)

### Chapter III: Production and Effect of Evidence (Sections 55-104)
**Types of Evidence**:
- Oral evidence (spoken testimony)
- Documentary evidence (written/electronic documents)
- Primary vs secondary evidence

**Important Sections**:
- **Section 55**: Oral evidence must be direct
- **Section 56**: Oral evidence of document contents
- **Section 62**: Primary evidence (originals)
- **Section 63**: Secondary evidence (copies, INCLUDING ELECTRONIC)
- **Section 88-93**: Electronic/digital records provisions (NEW FOCUS)

### Chapter IV: Documentary Evidence (Sections 74-90)
- Proof of documents and their execution
- Public documents vs private documents
- Presumptions about documents

**Important Sections**:
- **Section 74-78**: Public documents (government records, etc.)
- **Section 79-85**: Private documents (personal records)
- **Section 86-87**: Certified copies
- **Section 88**: Electronic records admissibility (CRITICAL)

### Chapter V: Burden of Proof (Sections 105-112)
- **Who must prove what**
- When burden shifts between parties
- Standard of proof in criminal vs civil cases

**Important Sections**:
- **Section 105**: Burden of proof (basic rule)
- **Section 106**: Burden on person with special knowledge
- **Section 108-111**: Specific situations (death, ownership, etc.)

### Chapter VI: Witnesses (Sections 113-135)
- Who can testify as witness
- Competency to testify
- Compellability (can be forced to testify)

**Important Sections**:
- **Section 113**: All persons competent unless stated otherwise
- **Section 114-117**: Who cannot testify (judges, certain officials)
- **Section 118**: Dumb witnesses, child witnesses
- **Section 119-134**: Examination procedures

### Chapter VII: Oral Evidence (Sections 136-141)
- How oral evidence must be given
- Hearsay rule and its exceptions

**Important Sections**:
- **Section 136**: Oral evidence must be direct
- **Section 137-141**: Hearsay exceptions (dying declarations, etc.)

### Chapter VIII: Improper Admission and Rejection of Evidence (Sections 142-143)
- Effect of wrongly admitting/rejecting evidence
- When it affects judgment

### Chapter IX: Judgments of Courts (Sections 144-151)
- When previous judgments are relevant
- Res judicata principles
- Conclusiveness of judgments

**Important Sections**:
- **Section 144-145**: Relevancy of previous judgments
- **Section 148**: Finality of certain judgments

### Chapter X: Opinions of Third Persons (Sections 152-162)
- When expert opinions admissible
- When non-expert opinions admissible

**Important Sections**:
- **Section 152**: Expert opinions (when relevant)
- **Section 153-154**: Who qualifies as expert
- **Section 157-161**: Non-expert opinions (handwriting, age, relationships)

### Chapter XI: Character (Sections 163-170)
- When character evidence is relevant
- Good character vs bad character

**Important Sections**:
- **Section 163**: Character generally inadmissible
- **Section 165-166**: Exceptions (accused's good character, victim's bad character)
- **Section 169**: Previous convictions relevant for credibility

## Digital Evidence Provisions (REVOLUTIONARY CHANGE)

### Section 63: Secondary Evidence (Includes Electronic)
**Now Includes**:
- Photocopies, scanned copies
- **Electronic copies stored on phones/computers**
- **Email forwards**
- **WhatsApp/SMS screenshots**
- **Cloud storage copies**
- Printouts of digital documents

**Major Change**: Electronic copies now explicitly part of secondary evidence definition

### Section 88-93: Electronic Records
**Section 88**: Electronic records admissible
- Same evidentiary value as paper documents
- No need for Section 65B certificate (IEA requirement REMOVED)
- Simplified process

**Section 89**: How to prove electronic records
- Can be proved like any other document
- Authentication still needed but simplified

**Section 90**: Presumption about electronic records
- If produced from proper custody, presumed genuine
- Burden on opposing party to prove otherwise

**Section 91**: Presumption about electronic signatures
- Digital signatures presumed genuine if properly certified

**Section 92**: Presumption about secure electronic records
- Records with digital signature presumed secure

**Section 93**: Presumption about electronic agreements
- Contracts in electronic form valid

1. **Communication Records**:
   - Emails (full thread)
   - WhatsApp chats and messages
   - SMS/text messages
   - Telegram, Signal messages
   - Social media DMs
   - Video call recordings

2. **Social Media Content**:
   - Facebook/Instagram/Twitter posts

3. **Digital Documents**:

4. **Financial Records**:
   - E-banking statements
   - Digital payment records
   - Cryptocurrency transactions

5. **Location & Tracking Data**:
   - GPS coordinates

6. **Audio-Visual Records**:

### Authentication Requirements for Digital Evidence

**Still Need**:
1. Proof of authenticity (who created it)
2. Chain of custody (who handled it)
3. Integrity (not tampered with)
4. Relevance to the case

**Tools for Authentication**:
- Hash values (digital fingerprints)
- Digital signatures
- Timestamps
- Metadata analysis
- Expert testimony
- Witness testimony (person who created/received it)

**Made Easier**: No longer need Section 65B certificate (complex paperwork requirement removed)

## Important Concepts

### Primary Evidence (Section 62)
- **Original document**
- For electronic: File in original storage location
- Best evidence

### Secondary Evidence (Section 63)
- **Copies of original**
- For electronic: Copies, printouts, screenshots
- Admissible when original not available or when electronic

### Hearsay Rule (Section 136)
- **General Rule**: Second-hand information not admissible
- Must be direct evidence from person who saw/heard/experienced
- **Exceptions**: Dying declarations, expert opinions, admissions, etc.

### Burden of Proof (Section 105-112)
**Criminal Cases**:
- Prosecution must prove guilt "beyond reasonable doubt"
- Accused presumed innocent
- Burden on prosecution

**Civil Cases**:
- Plaintiff must prove case on "preponderance of probabilities"
- Balance of probabilities standard

### Expert Evidence (Section 152-154)
**When Needed**:
- Scientific/technical matters
- Foreign law
- Handwriting analysis
- Medical opinions
- Digital forensics (NEW importance)

**Who Qualifies as Expert**:
- Specialized knowledge/skill
- Training and experience
- Court recognizes expertise

### Admissions vs Confessions
**Admissions** (Section 17-23):
- Statement suggesting fact in issue
- Can be made by anyone (party, agent)
- Civil and criminal cases
- Not conclusive proof

**Confessions** (Section 24-30):
- Statement admitting guilt
- Only in criminal cases
- Only by accused
- Can be conclusive if voluntary

### Confession to Police (Section 25-26)
**Critical Rule**: Confession to police officer NOT admissible
- Protects against coercion
- Exception: Information leading to discovery (Section 27)

### Dying Declaration (Section 137)
- Statement by person about cause of death
- Made when death is imminent
- Admissible as exception to hearsay
- No oath required
- Highly credible

## Presumptions in BSA

### Presumptions About Documents
- **Section 91**: Genuine documents presumed authentic
- **Section 92**: Certified copies presumed accurate
- **Section 93**: Electronic records presumed genuine

### Presumptions Favoring Accused
- Innocence presumed until proven guilty
- Doubt benefits accused

### Presumptions Against Accused (Specific Statutes)
- POCSO Act: Presumption of guilt
- NDPS Act: Presumption of conscious possession
- Prevention of Corruption: Presumption of guilt for public servants

## Practical Application

### In Criminal Trials
1. Prosecution presents evidence
2. Must be relevant (Chapter II)
3. Must be admissible (Chapters III-IV)
4. Witnesses examined (Chapter VI)
5. Expert evidence if needed (Chapter X)
6. Burden on prosecution (Chapter V)
7. Judge evaluates credibility
8. Judgment based on evidence beyond reasonable doubt

### In Civil Cases
1. Plaintiff bears initial burden
2. Evidence must be relevant and admissible
3. Documents and witnesses presented
4. Burden may shift to defendant
5. Preponderance of probabilities standard
6. Judge decides on balance

### For Digital Evidence
1. Collect digital evidence properly
2. Maintain chain of custody
3. Authenticate (hash values, witnesses)
4. Prove relevance
5. Submit under Section 88-93
6. No Section 65B certificate needed (BSA advantage)
7. Court evaluates authenticity and weight

## Important Case Law Principles (Pre-BSA, Still Relevant)

1. **Evidence must be relevant**: Irrelevant facts inadmissible
2. **Best evidence rule**: Original preferred over copies
3. **Hearsay inadmissible**: Unless exception applies
4. **Dying declaration reliable**: High evidentiary value
5. **Expert opinion not binding**: Court not bound to accept
6. **Circumstantial evidence**: Can convict if no other explanation
7. **Presumption of innocence**: Fundamental principle
8. **Benefit of doubt**: Goes to accused in criminal cases

## Advantages of BSA Over IEA

1. **Simpler Process**: Electronic evidence admission streamlined
2. **Modern Language**: Easier to understand
3. **Technology Ready**: Covers all digital formats
4. **Less Paperwork**: No Section 65B certificate
5. **Future-Proof**: Broad definitions cover emerging tech
6. **Practical**: Addresses real-world digital evidence scenarios

## Challenges and Limitations

1. **Authentication**: Still need to prove authenticity
2. **Digital Forensics**: Requires expertise
3. **Tampering Concerns**: Digital evidence can be manipulated
4. **Privacy Issues**: Balancing evidence needs with privacy rights
5. **Technical Knowledge**: Judges/lawyers need digital literacy
6. **Chain of Custody**: Critical but complex for digital evidence
